Finding a house

How Do I Begin My Home Search?

The most important step of buying a home is choosing a property that’s right for you. This involves several steps of research and due diligence. It's advisable to use a mix of resources for finding a home to stay informed.

Resources for Finding a Home

  • Online Realty Portals

  • Online/Offline Classifieds

  • Print Ads in Newspapers

  • Property Fairs / Exhibitions

  • Agents

  • Developers

  • Hoardings/Ads

  • Family/Friends

Location

Your quality of life will depend on the location you choose for your home. Choose a location based on:

  • Connectivity with your workplace: Ensure the location is well-connected to your place of work.

  • Ease of commute: Consider the availability of public transportation and road conditions.

  • Presence of basic amenities/markets: Check for nearby grocery stores, healthcare facilities, and other essential services.

  • Future appreciation prospects: Research the potential for property value appreciation in the area.

  • Social infrastructure: Look for schools, parks, and community centers that contribute to a good living environment.

  

Home Loans & Finance

The Loan Process


Step 1: Apply for a Home Loan and Get the Initial Evaluation Done
  • Submit application documents with a processing fee.
  • Bank evaluation based on credit history and documents.
Step 2: Bank Verification
  • If eligible for a loan, bring your documents to meet a bank official.
  • If not eligible, you will lose your processing fee.
  • Bank will verify all submitted documents.
  • A representative will visit your workplace and residence to cross-check details.
  • The property to be purchased will be evaluated.
  • The bank will evaluate your repayment abilities.
  • If approved, the bank will prepare an offer letter with conditions you should review.
Step 3: Bank Approvals
  • Re-read the document, and get a legal opinion too if you are unsure. There may be points that you can change or negotiate.
  • Once you are sure, sign the loan agreement.
Step 4: After Signing
  • Submit post-dated cheques.
  • The bank will disburse the loan as a lump sum payment (usually for a ready-to-move property) or in installments (for an under-construction flat).

Loan Document Checklist

There is a lot of paperwork involved in securing a loan. Here’s a checklist of the essential documents you’ll need:

  • Loan Application: Obtained from the bank or financial institution.

  • Processing Fee: Non-refundable, usually between 0.25% and 0.50% of the loan amount.

  • Photographs: Make sure you have at least three.

  • Identity Proof: PAN card, driver’s license, Aadhar card, or passport.

  • Address Proof: Driver’s license, passport, recent telephone or electricity bills, or Aadhar card.

  • Proof of Salaried Income: Copy of income tax returns (last three years) and original salary slips.

  • Proof of Business Existence (if Self-Employed): Relevant documentation showing business operations.

  • Income Tax Returns (if Self-Employed): 3 years of returns with computation of income.

  • CA Certified Balance Sheet and Profit & Loss Account (if Self-Employed): Financial statements for the last three years.

  • Advance Income Tax Payments (if Self-Employed): Photocopies of challans showing payment.

  • Bank Statements: Passbooks or statements of the last six months.

  • CIBIL (Credit) Score: Will be checked by the bank.

  • Acknowledgment Letter: After submitting the original documents, take an acknowledgment letter from the bank.


Loan Pre-Approval

There are advantages and disadvantages to getting a loan pre-approval:

Advantages

  • Better Negotiation: With pre-approval, you have a clearer idea of your buying capacity, which can enhance your negotiating power with the builder.
  • Faster Disbursement: Once you finalize a property, the process of actual disbursement becomes very quick.
  • Guidance from Bank: The bank can assist in deciding the right property in terms of legal paperwork and project approvals. However, you should still conduct your own checks.
  • Simplified Process: Only the property papers need to be submitted for the loan, and if everything is in order, the loan gets disbursed promptly.

Disadvantages

  • Validity Period: Pre-approval is typically valid for 3 to 6 months, depending on the bank. After this period, you may need to pay the processing fee again.
  • Project Approval Delays: If you finalize a project that is not approved by the bank, it may take additional time for the bank to approve the project. If rejected, you might need to find a new project or loan.

Loan Offer Letter

When the bank draws up an offer letter, consider these terms before signing the document:

Loan Amount Sanctioned: Verify the total amount of the loan that has been approved.
Interest Rate: Check the interest rate being offered.
Interest Rate Type: Determine whether the rate is floating, fixed, or a mix of both.
Rate Changes: Understand if and how the interest rate may change over time.
Disbursement Installments: Confirm the number of installments in which the bank will disburse the loan.
EMI Amount: Review the amount of the Equated Monthly Installment (EMI).
Loan Tenure: Know the duration over which the loan must be repaid.
Mode of Repayment: Clarify the method of repayment.
Schemes or Offers: Check for any applicable schemes or offers.
Terms and Conditions: Read the terms and conditions of the loan thoroughly.

About EMI

You repay a loan in monthly installments.

Each month you repay the loan by paying an Equated Monthly Instalment (EMI). The EMI consists of both the principal (the amount you borrowed) and interest (the fee the bank charges to loan the cash). Initially, your EMI will mostly be to repay the interest. As time passes, the principal repayment component increases until your loan is paid off. Your repayment plan affects your tax benefits since there are different tax benefits you can claim for interest and principal repayments. Banks consider the maximum EMI one can pay by using a factor called FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio). It’s usually between 50-60% of the net take-home salary. For example, if Priya has a Rs. 1 lakh monthly take-home salary, she can pay Rs. 60K of EMIs (combined for home, automobile, personal loan, etc.).

Interest Rates

Your interest on a loan consists of two parts: the Base Rate and the Spread.
Base Rate: This is the minimum lending rate below which banks cannot offer you a loan.
Spread: This is the final rate of interest after adding a percentage to the Base Rate. It is calculated based on factors such as tenure risk, credit loss, the profit requirement of the bank, operating costs, and the risk assigned to individual customers. While each bank’s Base Rate may be fixed for all customers, the Spread can vary, meaning you could end up paying higher or lower interest rates depending on the Spread charged by the bank. There is no cap on how much the Spread can be above the Base Rate.
Even though the RBI mandates that the Spread must be constant over the tenure of the loan, you should still check for any increases in your EMI. Verify whether any increase in EMI is due to a hike in the Base Rate or the Spread.

Choosing a House

Choosing A Location

Metropolitan cities’ real estate markets are no longer considered a safe haven for property investment. With a growing demand for a reduction in property rates, the chances of investing in metros are getting slim. It is becoming increasingly pertinent to explore destinations away from major cities and invest in upcoming areas. Property prices in metros are beyond the reach of the common man. However, areas beyond the cities offer larger homes with more affordable rates. Investors should think beyond metros due to lower prices, rapidly developing infrastructure, improved connectivity, and the high potential for appreciation. If you are looking at selecting a home in the affordable price bandwidth, you will need to keep the pros and cons in mind.


Site Visit Checklist

Use this checklist so you don't miss anything out on a site visit:

Take Photos: Try to visit a site during the worst time for a location, such as monsoons, to understand what the worst-case scenario might be.
Construction Quality: Check the ceiling and walls for cracks or moisture.
Interior Quality: Assess the quality of fittings such as flooring and cabinets.
Foundation: Look for cracks on the outer walls, which may indicate poor construction.
Official Inspection: Request the results of the last building inspection.
Structural Engineer: Consider hiring a structural engineer to assess the building's integrity.
Lifts: Gauge the waiting time for lifts during busy periods.
Power: Ensure there is emergency power backup in case of outages.
Water Supply: Confirm if the water supply is available 24/7.
Wiring: Test each switch to ensure it is functioning properly.
Phone Signal: Check if your phone signal is clear in all areas.
Plumbing: Flush the toilets and run the taps to check water pressure and plumbing quality.
Leaks: Look for leaks from walls, ceilings, under sinks, and on pipes.
Flood Risk: Assess the potential for damage due to moisture and how transit is affected.
Safety: Verify if the building has security measures like guards or cameras and inquire about any break-ins.
Timing: Observe how light, noise, etc., vary throughout the day.

Negotiating

Never accept a deal without negotiating. Here are some negotiation skills:

Here are a few bargaining tips that come in handy when negotiating on property price:
Don’t Divulge Too Much: Avoid sharing too many details or personal thoughts about the property.
Ask the Right Questions: Inquire if the seller is in a hurry to sell and how many other buyers are interested in the property.
Identify Property Drawbacks: Look for any potential issues the agent might overlook or downplay.
Get Expert Help: Consult a professional or a knowledgeable friend to help you assess properties.
Research Thoroughly: Don’t assume the agent is completely transparent; do your own research.
Start Low: Begin negotiations with a figure lower than your actual budget and be ready to compromise.
Check Local Prices: Talk to residents in the area to get an idea of local prices.
Additional Resources: Housing.com offers a property valuation tool to check neighborhood demand and current market value, allowing you to compare with nearby homes.

What If Delays

What should I do if there is a construction delay?

In the case of an extended delay by the developer, the buyer has several legal recourses:
Legal Action: Under the Consumer Protection Act, a buyer can file a case against the developer. The court may issue an order directing the developer to complete the project within a specified timeframe. Failure to comply may result in further legal action under the criminal procedure code.
Civil Lawsuit: If a project stalls or the developer delays possession, the buyer can file a civil lawsuit to seek a refund of the invested money.

Developer Reputation

Ask the right questions, search previous news articles, and ask pointed questions to assess a developer’s reputation. Here are some steps you can take:
Legal Qualifications: Ensure that the developer has the necessary legal pre-qualifications and due diligence documents.
Track Record: Check the developer’s track record by visiting previous projects and asking about the number of completed and ongoing projects.
Project Timeliness: Determine if previous projects were completed on time.
Research Forums: Research real estate forums like IREF for feedback and reviews.
Builder Affiliations: Check if the builder is affiliated with industry associations such as the Confederation of Real Estate Developers Association of India (CREDAI) or the Builder’s Association of India (BAI).
Online Search: Search for articles about the builder and their previous projects on the Internet.
Financial Approval: Verify if the project is pre-approved by reputable financial institutions.

Hidden Costs and Taxes

Hidden Costs and Taxes in Home Buying

Home buying requires a high level of financial preparedness. In places like Mumbai, Bengaluru, Delhi-NCR, and other metro cities, the EMI on a home loan is usually very high due to higher property prices. Therefore, if you are financially not ready, your whole plan of buying a home could go awry. Ensure you have the eligibility to get a loan and are financially ready to not just pay the EMI but also raise the margin money.

Regular Expenses When Buying a Home

Each month, you will repay the loan by paying an Equated Monthly Installment (EMI). The EMI consists of both the principal (the amount you borrowed) and interest (the fee the bank charges to loan the cash). Initially, your EMI will mostly be to repay the interest. As time passes, the principal repayment component increases until your loan is paid off. Your repayment plan affects your tax benefits since there are different tax benefits you can claim for interest and principal repayment.

Taxes and Additional Costs
  • Stamp Duty: Usually 5% of the property value (and a penalty of 2% every month if not paid). For more information, visit Stamp Duty during Property Registration.
  • Registration: About 1% of the property value, subject to a maximum of Rs. 30,000. This should be done within four months from the date of implementation, after paying the stamp duty. For more details, check Laws Related to Registration of Property Transactions in India.
  • Brokerage: Usually between 1% and 2% of the transaction value. For more information, visit Choosing a Licensed Broker.
  • VAT / Cess: Paid by the buyer and can be levied retrospectively. VAT is paid to cover materials used for construction. As of April 1, 2015, service tax is charged at 14% on a portion of the sale value, depending on the size and cost of the house. For properties less than Rs. 1 crore, it’s 3.5% of the price; for properties over Rs. 1 crore, it’s 4.2%, but this varies by state. Facilities such as parking and maintenance of amenities will also draw service tax. However, single residential units like an independent villa or bungalow are exempted from service tax. If the developer has completed a project and obtained all required approvals, including an Occupancy Certificate (OC) before selling an apartment, there isn’t any service involved, so no service tax or VAT is applicable.
  • TDS (Tax Deducted at Source): The buyer must deduct a tax of 1% on the entire amount for properties valued at Rs. 50 lakh or more; this can go up to 20% if the seller doesn’t share their PAN details. If payment is made in installments, tax has to be deducted with each installment. TDS is also applicable if you’ve bought an under-construction project on or before June 1, 2013, and where part payment is outstanding, it must be deducted on the outstanding amount.

Second Home Buyers

What Should I Consider Before Buying a Second Home?
  • Do a thorough check on the developer’s credentials and reputation. Seek opinions from discussion forums such as IREF.
  • Find out whether leading financial institutions have approved the project and are willing to finance buyers.
  • Banks may offer less financing on a second home, meaning higher upfront payment.
  • Since lenders see it as an investment, they may give you a higher interest rate.
  • If you are already paying off loans, the loan amount you’ll get will be smaller.
  • Plan for situations that may hamper your ability to pay your EMIs. A good rule to follow is that your property investments shouldn’t account for more than 50% of the total value of your assets.
  • Check the tax section for tax implications—these are different from those on your first home.
  • Consider what’s attractive to renters if you plan to rent it out—neighbourhood lifestyle, connectivity, furnishing, and maintenance all add to the costs.
